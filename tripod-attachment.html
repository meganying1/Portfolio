Tripod Attachment

Designing and manufacturing a tripod attachment to alleviate discomfort and enhance usability for individuals with cerebral palsy and wrist tendonitis.

Abstract

Throughout this project, I worked with a team to design assistive technology for a specific population. We chose to create a tripod attachment that could be used by those with cerebral palsy or wrist tendonitis to take photos. We were given the task of fabricating a prototype of the product given a budget of $200. In addition, we needed to consider the mass manufacturing process for our design.

Design Process

We began by creating a user survey. Through our interviews, we identified that small mechanisms of the tripod, such as wheels and levers, were most difficult to use for our target population. Thus, we focused on eliminating the need to screw a camera into a tripod, as well as the need to adjust knobs for the swivel and tilt features. Our team brainstormed various mechanisms for each function. We consulted with faculty and ranked each mechanism using metrics to select our final design.

Product Features

Our final product is an attachment that can be screwed into any tripod. It is capable of automatically securing a phone, preventing the user from needing to apply any extraneous force and reducing the need for steady hands. The base of our product, which is made of a rigid material known as ABS, houses an IR sensor, which can detect when a phone is being held over it. A pair of wings powered by micro servos, a nano board, and a 9V battery then open and close to allow the user to place a phone in the slot. Once the user has finished taking photos, the phone can be removed by pulling it out of the subassembly. The slot of the base is lined with foam and the wings are made of TPU, a flexible material. This allows our product to accommodate phones of various widths and lengths.

Our design also includes an interface piece attached under the base. The inner interface is made of TPU, while the outer portion is made of ABS. Both components have rounded gear teeth for easier swiveling and locking. As a result, the user can twist the subassembly without needing to loosen any knobs. We also included holes for magnets in our design. These magnets cause the inner and outer pieces to snap into place, while allowing the parts to be easily dismantled.

Analysis Process

Our team performed hand calculations, as well as an FEA simulation, on the wings of our product. We chose to have a high factor of safety to ensure that the product is durable, and thus, can be used many times before needing to be replaced. We also made cost estimations for our product by submitting a quote of our components to Protolabs using a sample quantity of 50. Although our prototype was fabricated using 3D printing, we decided that our mass manufacturing method would be injection molding. This method is very efficient and therefore, more parts can be created at a lower price for each. Based on our calculations, our product would cost approximately $58 to manufacture and assemble. Thus, we decided to price our attachment at around $62 to account for a 7% profit.

Results

My team was able to fabricate a functioning model of our tripod attachment. Through this project, I developed a better understanding of the different factors that go into mass manufacturing of a product, including material selection and manufacturing methods. I also gained hands-on experience with incorporating electronics into mechanical systems.
