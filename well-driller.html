Pedal-Powered Well Driller

Developing a conceptual design of a well driller that relies on human-produced pedal power.

Abstract

This project involved working in a team of three to design a modification of the Village Drill (https://villagedrill.com/) that relies on human-produced pedal power. Our goal was to create the optimal design by considering equilibrium, power transmission, failure criteria, and specific community needs.

Design Process

My team started our design process by brainstorming mechanisms to convert the rotational motion of pedaling into the vertical motion of driller, as well as how they would be incorporated into our system level designs. The ideas included using a pile driver, a bevel gear, or a worm gear. Our team then selected the pile driver mechanism based on the stakeholders, specific customer needs, and target specifications of our design. The pile driver works by lifting up the driller and dropping it.

Analysis Process

Once our final design was selected, we used SolidWorks to create a model of our final design. Based on the properties and dimensions of our model, we were able to calculate the required tension in the cable, force applied to the pedal, and reaction forces at fixed points in the structure. Based on the reaction forces we solved for, we determined the minimum pin diameters required to achieve a satisfactory factor of safety. We also performed a finite element analysis of the pedal. Finally, we solved for the power required to operate our design, and thus, the required pedal rotation rate.

Results

By analyzing our calculations, my team found that our final design was reasonable and efficient. This project provided me an opportunity to apply mechanical principles, such as statics, stress analysis, and dynamics, to a real-world issue. By completing it, I was able to learn which factors must be considered during the design process, as well as what metrics can be used to determine the success of a product. I also became more comfortable using CAD and FEA.
